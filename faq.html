Expanded content for index.html (add below existing sections) ### Timing for Interval Training (HIIT) Interval training relies on precise, repeatable work-to-rest ratios — 30 seconds on, 15 seconds off, for example. A stopwatch with lap tracking lets you log each round without breaking focus, so you can review your splits afterward instead of watching the clock mid-set. To use StopwatchPro for intervals: 1. Decide your work and rest durations before you start (e.g., 40s work / 20s rest). 2. Press Lap at the end of each work segment and again at the end of each rest segment — this gives you two lap entries per round. 3. After your session, scan the lap list: work segments that run long usually mean fatigue is setting in, which is useful feedback for pacing future workouts. If you train the same interval structure often, a dedicated interval timer with audio cues (see our Interval Timer tool) can be faster than manually lapping — but the stopwatch is ideal when your intervals vary round to round, like fartlek runs or coached circuits. ### Timing Study Sessions (Pomodoro Method) The Pomodoro Technique breaks work into focused 25-minute blocks separated by 5-minute breaks, with a longer break after four blocks. A stopwatch works for this if you're comfortable manually starting/stopping at each transition, but most people find a countdown-style Pomodoro timer easier since it alerts you automatically (see our Pomodoro Timer tool below). Where the stopwatch does help even in Pomodoro-style work: logging how long you actually spent in flow before your mind wandered. Runners and writers alike sometimes lap every time they lose and regain focus, then review afterward. ### Timing Science Experiments Lab timing often needs millisecond precision and a clear, unambiguous record of splits — reaction rates, titration intervals, or repeated-trial consistency checks. StopwatchPro's high-resolution timer (accurate to the millisecond via your browser's performance timing API) and lap log make it straightforward to record multiple trials in one continuous session, then export or transcribe the split list afterward. ### Coaching and Race Timing Coaches timing multiple athletes on a single watch can use laps to separate individual splits, then read the "Total" column to reconstruct each athlete's finishing time. This works well for informal meets and practice sessions; sanctioned races generally require certified timing equipment. ### A Short History of the Stopwatch Mechanical stopwatches date back to the mid-1800s, originally developed for horse racing and later adopted by factories for time-and-motion studies. Digital stopwatches arrived in the 1970s with quartz movements, pushing accuracy from roughly a fifth of a second down to hundredths. Browser-based stopwatches like this one use your device's high-resolution performance clock, which is generally accurate to within a few milliseconds — precise enough for virtually any everyday timing need. ### Troubleshooting **The timer seems to drift or lag on my device.** This is almost always caused by the browser tab being backgrounded or the device entering a low-power state; keep the tab active and the screen on for the most accurate readings. **My laps disappeared after I refreshed the page.** Lap data is stored in memory for the current session only and clears on refresh. If you need a persistent log, note your splits down before reloading. **Keyboard shortcuts aren't responding.** Make sure the page (not the address bar or another element) has focus — click anywhere on the stopwatch area first. --- ## 2. Expanded FAQ (faq.html) **Does StopwatchPro work without an internet connection?** Yes, once the page has loaded once, the stopwatch continues to function offline since all timing happens locally in your browser. **Can I use StopwatchPro on my phone?** Yes. The layout is responsive and the Start/Lap/Reset controls are large enough for touch use. Keyboard shortcuts apply only on devices with a physical or connected keyboard. **Is there a limit to how many laps I can record?** No — you can record laps for as long as the browser tab stays open and active. **Does StopwatchPro collect or store my timing data?** No. All timing happens client-side in your browser; nothing is sent to a server or saved after you close or refresh the tab. See our Privacy Policy for details. **Can I run multiple stopwatches at once in different tabs?** Yes, each browser tab runs its own independent instance. **Why does the millisecond display sometimes skip numbers?** Browsers update the display roughly 60 times per second, so at very fast refresh rates you may see the millisecond counter skip by more than 1 between renders — the underlying elapsed-time measurement itself remains accurate. --- ## 3.
